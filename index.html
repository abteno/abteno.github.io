The exploration and manipulation of unstructured and dynamic environments have long been a cornerstone of robotics research. Autonomous rovers capable of performing complex tasks are essential in scenarios ranging from space exploration to disaster recovery, where human intervention is limited or impossible. The goal of this project was to develop a rover system capable of completing three primary objectives:

  • Panel Operation and Feedback Observation: The rover needed to operate a variety of elements on a control panel, including toggling switches, measuring electrical parameters, setting controls, and interpreting visual feedback indicators.
  • Probe Placement and Retrieval: The rover had to identify designated locations from a provided map, retrieve probes from an onboard storage unit, and place them accurately in these locations, demonstrating spatial awareness and dexterous manipulation.
  • Autonomous Navigation: The rover needed to traverse complex environments semi-autonomously and, eventually, fully autonomously, gathering environmental data along its route to aid decision-making.

These capabilities are critical in situations like extraterrestrial exploration missions or hazardous environments where a high degree of precision, adaptability, and autonomy is required.

  • Control Panel Interaction: The rover was equipped with tactile sensors to determine switch positions and rotary encoder feedback to measure the precise movement of dials. Electrical parameter sensors (e.g., voltmeters) were integrated for real-time measurements.
  • Navigation Sensors: LIDAR, cameras, and ultrasonic sensors were combined to create a robust Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) system. GPS modules augmented outdoor navigation.
  • Indicator Observation: A high-resolution camera was used to read visual indicators such as LEDs or analog gauges, with a vision-based feedback recognition algorithm.

A simulated environment mimicking the final deployment scenario was developed to test the roverâ€™s performance. Iterative testing cycles focused on refining mechanical accuracy, improving autonomy algorithms, and validating sensor feedback systems.

Results

The rover successfully demonstrated the ability to complete all objectives in controlled and semi-structured environments. Key outcomes included:

Initial testing showed partial success in fully autonomous operation, with an 85% completion rate in traversing complex paths. Aerial Vehicle for Mars

Design Feb 23, 2022

This project was developed as part of the International Planetary Aerial Systems Challenge (IPASC), a prestigious design competition organized by the Mars Society South Asia (MSSA). The challenge required designing an astronaut-assistive Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) tailored for Mars operations. This UAV had to be fully equipped and mission-ready, capable of performing critical tasks in Mars' unique atmospheric conditions. The project was an intensive exercise in multidisciplinary design, requiring careful planning and optimization of subsystems while adhering to the constraints imposed by Mars' thin atmosphere and challenging terrain.

Introduction

Mars exploration presents a range of challenges, including its low atmospheric pressure, extreme temperature fluctuations, and rugged terrain. These conditions necessitate innovative solutions to support astronauts during surface missions. The objective of this project was to design a UAV capable of assisting astronauts by:

  • Providing aerial reconnaissance and mapping of Martian terrain.
  • Transporting lightweight payloads such as tools or samples.
  • Monitoring environmental conditions to ensure astronaut safety.
  • Performing autonomous operations to minimize astronaut workload.

To achieve these goals, the UAV design had to account for Mars' thin atmosphere (approximately 1% of Earthâ€™s atmospheric density), reduced gravity (about 38% of Earthâ€™s gravity), and harsh environmental conditions.

Methods

The project was executed in several phases, each focusing on the development and integration of specific UAV subsystems.

Aerodynamic Design

Given Mars' thin atmosphere, achieving sufficient lift was a primary design challenge. Key aerodynamic considerations included:

  • Wing Design: A high aspect ratio fixed-wing configuration was chosen to maximize lift-to-drag ratio. The wings were equipped with advanced airfoils specifically designed for low Reynolds number flow conditions.
  • Propulsion System: Electric propulsion was selected for its reliability and efficiency in low-density air. High RPM motors with large-diameter, low-pitch propellers were used to generate adequate thrust.
  • Stability Enhancements: Stabilizers and control surfaces were designed to ensure smooth and stable flight, even in gusty conditions.

Structural Design

The UAV's structure needed to be lightweight yet robust to withstand Marsâ€™ environmental stressors. Design features included:

  • Material Selection: Carbon fiber composites were used for the frame and wings to minimize weight while ensuring strength.
  • Payload Bay: A modular payload compartment was integrated to accommodate scientific instruments, sensors, or small cargo.
  • Thermal Insulation: The structure was insulated to protect sensitive components from temperature extremes.

Avionics and Autonomy

The UAV was equipped with advanced avionics systems to enable autonomous operation and reliable communication in Mars' environment:

  • Navigation System: Inertial Measurement Units (IMUs), GPS simulators, and vision-based SLAM were integrated to ensure precise navigation in GPS-denied environments.
  • Autonomous Control: AI-driven algorithms were implemented for obstacle avoidance, path planning, and task prioritization.
  • Communication: A long-range radio-frequency communication system was designed to maintain connectivity with astronaut teams or base stations.

Testing and Validation

A scaled prototype was developed for Earth-based testing, simulating Mars-like conditions wherever possible:

  • Wind tunnel tests were conducted to validate aerodynamic performance under low-density air conditions.
  • Environmental chambers simulated Martian temperatures to test the thermal insulation and component durability.
  • Flight trials were performed in high-altitude areas with thin air to approximate Martian atmospheric conditions.

Results

The UAV successfully demonstrated its capability to meet the design objectives. Key results included:

  • Lift Generation: The high aspect ratio wings and optimized propellers provided sufficient lift, with performance exceeding initial predictions by 15%.
  • Payload Capacity: The UAV could carry payloads up to 5 kg, making it suitable for various astronaut-assistive tasks.
  • Autonomy: Autonomous navigation and task execution were achieved with 95% reliability during simulated missions.
  • Durability: The UAV withstood simulated Martian environmental conditions, including temperature cycles and low-density air.

Discussion and Conclusion

This project demonstrated the feasibility of designing a UAV for Mars operations by leveraging innovative aerodynamic, structural, and autonomous systems. The UAV's success in simulated Mars-like conditions validates its potential for real-world deployment in future exploration missions.

The design approach showcased the importance of multidisciplinary collaboration, combining expertise in aerodynamics, materials science, electronics, and artificial intelligence. The UAV's modularity and scalability make it adaptable to various mission requirements, from reconnaissance and sample collection to environmental monitoring.

Future enhancements could include incorporating solar panels for extended operational range, improving battery efficiency, and optimizing AI algorithms for even greater autonomy. These developments would further enhance the UAV's utility in supporting human exploration of Mars and other extraterrestrial environments.

Deep Learning Approach to Finite Element Analysis

Research Dec 12, 2023

This project explores the integration of Deep Learning with Finite Element Analysis (FEA) to develop a surrogate modeling framework, significantly reducing computational overhead while maintaining accuracy. Specifically, the research focuses on Bioprosthetic Heart Valves (BHVs), addressing challenges in their design and analysis using Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). The work has far-reaching implications for personalized medical applications and efficient engineering solutions.

Introduction

Finite Element Analysis (FEA) is critical for understanding mechanical properties like stress and strain but is often computationally prohibitive for real-time decision-making. This research introduces a Deep Learning-based FEA (DLFEA) framework to accelerate simulations while preserving accuracy. Using GNNs, this framework predicts deformation and coaptation metrics in Bioprosthetic Heart Valves (BHVs), essential for their efficient design and performance optimization.

Methods

The methodology involved dataset preparation, baseline modeling, and implementing advanced GNN architectures.

Dataset Preparation

  • Data sourced from 18,000 simulations of BHVs conducted using Isogeometric Analysis (IGA), capturing deformation under varying physiological conditions.
  • Parameters included valve thickness, material stiffness, and transvalvular pressure. Data split: Training (60%), Validation (20%), Testing (20%).

Baseline Model

  • Implemented a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) as a baseline, leveraging NURBS-aware convolutions for geometrical accuracy.

GNN Architectures

  • Graph Convolution Networks (GCNs): Aggregated local node features to predict deformations.
  • MeshGraphNets: Specialized in handling complex mesh-based data, integrating both local and global dependencies for superior performance.

Training and Evaluation

  • Models trained using the Adam optimizer with a batch size of 512.
  • Evaluation metrics included R2 Score, Euclidean Distance, Hausdorff Distance, and Procrustes Distance.

Results

  • R2 Scores: CNN: 0.9328, GCN: 0.9492, MeshGraphNets: 0.9589.
  • Distance Metrics:
    • Euclidean Distance: MeshGraphNets (0.0148) < GCN (0.0160) < CNN (0.0173).
    • Hausdorff Distance: MeshGraphNets (0.0712) < GCN (0.0772) < CNN (0.0830).
    • Procrustes Distance: MeshGraphNets (0.0099) < GCN (0.0120) < CNN (0.0210).

Discussion and Conclusion

The DLFEA framework demonstrated substantial improvements in computational efficiency and accuracy for modeling BHV deformation. MeshGraphNets consistently outperformed other architectures, highlighting the value of preserving spatial topology in complex simulations. Future enhancements include dynamic valve simulations, fatigue life prediction, and hybrid model integration to optimize performance.

This research provides a robust foundation for accelerating computational mechanics, especially in biomedical applications, enabling real-time, patient-specific solutions that enhance surgical outcomes and design efficiency.
